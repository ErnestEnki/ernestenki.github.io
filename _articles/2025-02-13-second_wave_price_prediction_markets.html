Startups and SMEs Public Markets

Currently, startups and small-to-medium enterprises (SMEs) are not allowed to be publicly traded due to the risks involved for investors and the broader market. These risks include low trading volumes, rapid changes in financial conditions, and limited transparency. Such barriers also make it harder for startups to secure partnerships with suppliers, distributors, banks, and other key stakeholders, further complicating their survival.

However, if Prediction Markets can help meaningfully reduce these risks, the benefits of allowing startups and SMEs to participate in public markets could be transformative‚Äîdriving more innovation, job creation, and economic dynamism.

Solving Low Liquidity Through Tight Trading Windows

A major concern in opening public markets to smaller companies is low liquidity, which can lead to extreme price fluctuations and poor price discovery. One potential solution is concentrating liquidity in scheduled trading windows.

For example:

  • Startups could trade only on Wednesday afternoons.
  • Small companies could trade on Tuesdays through Thursdays.
  • Medium-sized companies could trade every weekday afternoon.
  • Once a company graduates to large-cap status, it would enter the full trading schedule like traditional public companies.

By limiting the frequency of trading, liquidity would be naturally concentrated, reducing price instability while still allowing smaller companies to access public investment capital.

Investor Education as a Prerequisite for Participation

A common concern is that uninformed investors might take excessive risks or fall victim to scams when investing in smaller companies. Instead of relying on heavy-handed restrictions, a behavioral economics approach could be used to educate and empower investors.

For example, before being allowed to invest in startups and SMEs, investors could be required to:

  • Complete an online course on risk management, diversification, and consulting prediction markets before investing.
  • Answer a short quiz monthly to maintain their eligibility for placing new investments.

This would serve as a gentle "nudge" toward informed investing‚Äîproviding safeguards without the excessive micromanagement of many existing regulations, such as only allowing Accredited Investors to participate.

Risk Reduction Through Index Funds & ETFs

Another way to mitigate risk is by creating index funds and ETFs that hold baskets of startup and SME stocks. Investors would then be able to gain exposure to early-stage companies while still diversifying their risk across multiple businesses.

This approach would:

  • Provide a structured way for investors to enter the startup space without betting everything on individual companies.
  • Reduce volatility by spreading risk across many smaller firms.
  • Encourage long-term investment in high-growth sectors, benefiting both companies and investors.

By leveraging prediction markets, scheduled trading, investor education, and diversified funds, the risks of allowing startups and SMEs into public markets could be managed effectively. If executed well, this shift could unlock tremendous economic benefits, creating a more inclusive, innovative, and dynamic financial ecosystem.
