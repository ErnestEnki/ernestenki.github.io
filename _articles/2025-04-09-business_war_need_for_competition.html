Similarities between business and war

Business and war share a core purpose: they both compete for resources:

  • Businessess compete via creation, by selling produts and services for money, which they can use to purchase the resources they desire.
  • War competes via destruction, by threatening or causing damage to force other parties to hand over the resources they desire.

I posit that military competition arises from the lack of business competition, as the lack a healthy job market leads to few and poorly paid jobs, stressing the population, who become more prone to demagogues, overreactions, taking offenses; which ultimately lead to war.

Secondly, we posit a bad job market is largely the result of a stricly enforced patent system, which allows big companies to form oligopolies, by preemptively patents useful technologies, buidling huge patent portfolios, which tehy use to kill SMEs, which comprise a staggering 99% of businesses and contribute 60% of employment in OECD countries.

Interestingly, the military and the marketplace share many parallels, not only in the big picture, but also the very mechanisms for job creation.

  • In the military, when a new enemy appears, governments always find money for bombs and warplanes, even if they previously claimed to have no funds for essentials like schools and hospitals. Ultimately, this surge of activity generates many jobs.
    Similarly, in a true free market (without patents), when a formidable competitor emerges, companies suddenly allocate funds for large R&D projects. They offer salary raises to retain key employees and invest in expensive marketing campaigns, even if they previously claimed to have no money for basics like cost-of-living raises or replacing employees who had left—while still rewarding shareholders handsomely. Ultimately, this surge of activity generates many jobs.
  • In the military, governments employ many intelligence staff to gather enemy information, develop military strategies, spread propaganda, and try to recruit enemies to their side. Ultimately, this surge of activity generates many jobs.
    Similarly, in a true free market (without patents), companies employ numerous staff in intelligence: gathering competitor information, creating market strategies, engaging in marketing, and attempting to recruit competitors' staff. Ultimately, this surge of activity generates many jobs.
  • In the military, a vast number of soldiers serve as the tip of the spear, pushing into enemy territory to achieve victory. Ultimately, this surge of activity generates many jobs.
    Similarly, in a true free market (without patents), a vast number of salespeople, evangelists, public relations managers, and others act as the tip of the spear in the marketplace, pushing into competitor markets to gain industry dominance. Ultimately, this surge of activity generates many jobs.
  • In the military, there is a need for engineers, researchers, and scientists to develop new technologies to defeat the enemy. Ultimately, this surge of activity generates many jobs.
    Similarly, in a true free market (without patents), companies likewise create numerous positions for engineers, researchers, and scientists to develop new technologies to outperform competitors. Ultimately, this surge of activity generates many jobs.
  • In the military, enemy bombs destroy a country’s assets such as bridges, roads, and airplanes. This creates the need for cleanup, recycling of what remains, and reconstruction of replacements. Ultimately, this surge of activity generates many jobs.
    Similarly, in a true free market (without patents), competitors' innovations destroy the market relevance of a company’s assets such as products, services, internal tools and machinery, and marketing strategies; by making them obsolete. This creates the need for cleanup, recycling of what remains, and reconstruction of replacements that are more market relevant. Ultimately, this surge of activity generates many jobs.

Much academic research went into answers, which coalesced around financial system fragility and policy failures. However I posit there was another major factor: strict enforcment of patents.

The latest and today the most extensively used instrument of monopoly control is the patent privilege. …

The shaky patent was therefore never contested, and the public was gouged to the tune of $150,000,000 a year. Moreover, when the price is fixed on a necessity the actual cost goes beyond the direct cost. If you are an average citizen getting $2000 a year or less and the Ethyl Corporation adds $25 to your gasoline bill, the only place you can get that $25 is from your food budget. All this happens when patent cartels control the price of necessities...

Where necessities are involved, there is no brake on this tendency, and exploitation by patent control is most effective in necessities like housing, fuel, drugs, and basic materials.
